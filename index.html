Insights & Guides

Learn about carbon metrics and why these specific changes matter.

Carbon dioxide equivalent (CO₂e) is a unit used to compare the emissions from various greenhouse gases on the basis of their global warming potential (GWP), by converting amounts of other gases to the equivalent amount of carbon dioxide with the same global warming impact. For instance, methane is 25-28 times more potent than carbon dioxide over a 100-year timescale, so 1 tonne of methane equals 28 tonnes of CO₂e.

We use standard emission factors derived from environmental agencies (like US EPA and UK DEFRA). Private cars emit varying amounts of CO₂ based on fuel source (e.g., petrol/diesel cars average ~300g per mile, hybrids emit ~180g, and EVs emit ~70-90g depending on the average electric grid emissions of the electricity tariff used to charge them). Flight emissions include radiative forcing (heat trapping in high altitude) which compounds high atmospheric emissions.

Beef has a massive carbon footprint due to enteric fermentation (methane produced by cattle digestion), land-use change (deforestation to create pasture), and lower feed conversion efficiency. Producing 1kg of beef results in roughly 60-99kg of CO₂e, whereas 1kg of poultry is around 6kg of CO₂e, and root vegetables/lentils are generally under 1kg of CO₂e.
